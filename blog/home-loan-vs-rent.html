Home Loan vs Rent — Which is Better in India in 2026?

📅 June 2026 ⏱️ 8 min read 🏠 Home Loan Updated monthly
Quick answer: At current rates, buying makes sense if you plan to stay 7+ years, the property price is under 20× annual rent, and you have a stable income. Renting wins for the first 3–5 years purely on cost — but buying builds equity that renting never does.

The question millions of Indians face every year

You're paying ₹18,000/month rent in Bengaluru. Your colleagues say "stop paying rent — that money goes to the owner." Your parents say "buy a home — best investment." Your friends say "prices are too high — wait." Everyone has an opinion. Almost nobody does the actual math.

This article does the math. We compare home loan EMI vs rent for real Indian cities in 2026, factor in the opportunity cost of the down payment, and give you a decision framework.

Scenario: ₹60 Lakh apartment, 3 cities

Let's use a realistic scenario — a ₹60 lakh 2BHK apartment in three cities. Current home loan rate: 8.75% per annum (SBI, HDFC, Axis — June 2026). Tenure: 20 years.

Mumbai (suburbs)₹60L₹12L₹48L₹42,436₹22,000

The EMI is always higher than rent — for a ₹60L apartment at 8.75%. This is the starting point. But this comparison is incomplete without four more factors.

Factor 1 — The opportunity cost of the down payment

You pay ₹12L as down payment. If you had invested that ₹12L in a mutual fund at 12% annual return instead, it would become ₹38.2L in 10 years and ₹1.18 crore in 20 years.

When you "buy" using your down payment, you give up this growth. This is the hidden cost of buying that most people ignore.

Monthly cost of opportunity: ₹12L invested at 12% = ₹1,200/month of foregone returns in year 1, growing to ₹4,800/month by year 10. Add this to your EMI to get the true monthly cost of owning.

Factor 2 — Rent increases over time, EMI doesn't

Your rent rises 5–8% each year. In Bengaluru, a flat renting at ₹18,000 today will likely cost ₹29,000 in 7 years (at 7% annual increase). Your home loan EMI stays fixed at ₹42,436 for the full 20 years — it never increases.

At 7% annual rent increase in Bengaluru, the break-even point is around Year 13. After Year 13, the EMI is cheaper than rent — and in Year 21, the loan is fully paid and your housing cost drops to zero (only maintenance).

Factor 3 — You build equity, not just pay housing cost

Every EMI payment has two parts — interest and principal repayment. The principal part is not a "cost" — it is savings. In year 1, about ₹7,000 of your ₹42,436 EMI is principal repayment (equity building). By year 10, this rises to ₹15,000/month of equity building.

Over 20 years, you pay ₹1.02 crore in EMIs and own a property. The renter pays ₹70–80L in rent over the same period and owns nothing.

Factor 4 — Property appreciation

Indian residential property has appreciated at 5–8% annually in major cities over the last 15 years. A ₹60L apartment bought in 2026 could be worth ₹1.5–2 crore in 2045. Renters don't benefit from this appreciation.

🏆 Verdict: When to Buy vs Rent

✅ Buy if:
  • You plan to stay in the city for 7+ years
  • Property price is under 20× annual rent
  • You have stable income and 20% down payment ready
  • CIBIL is 700+ for best rates
  • You value stability and ownership
🏠 Rent if:
  • You may change cities within 5 years
  • Property price is over 25× annual rent (overpriced)
  • Income is variable or uncertain
  • You can invest the difference in equity SIPs
  • Better properties available in same budget to rent

The price-to-rent ratio — quick check

Divide property price by annual rent. Under 20 = buying is reasonable. Over 25 = renting is cheaper. This is called the Price-to-Rent ratio.

Bottom line

Buying a home is not always better than renting, and renting is not always "throwing money away." The right answer depends on your city, tenure, income stability, and what you do with the money you save by renting.
